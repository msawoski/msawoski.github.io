## Abstract

The Red/Blue button thought experiment recently went viral, prompting strongly polarized reactions. We analyze it through the pivotal-voter framework and derive a closed-form expression for the indifference threshold $u^*_n(\bar{v}, \tau)$, defined as the ratio of self-utility to per-other-utility at which an agent is indifferent between voting red and voting blue. The threshold has three asymptotic regimes: $\sqrt{n}$ growth at the symmetric point with known voter beliefs (recovering the bulk-regime calculation circulating informally), an $n$-independent asymptote

$$ u^{*}_\infty(\bar{v}, \tau) = \frac{1}{2\tau} \cdot \frac{\varphi(\zeta)}{\Phi(-\zeta)} $$

when the population's blue-leaning rate is itself uncertain (where $\zeta = (\bar{v} - 1/2)/\tau$ is the standardized distance from the prior mean to the knife edge, and $\varphi, \Phi$ are the standard normal PDF and CDF), and a sharp crossover between them at $n \sim 1/(4\tau^2)$. The closed-form $u^{\*}_\infty$ specializes a result of Edlin–Gelman–Kaplan to the Red/Blue puzzle and exhibits the dominant role played by uncertainty $\tau$ over the central estimate $\bar{v}$. We also analyze a small-n regime in which particular attachments to specific individuals can flip the decision. The unified formula $u^{\*}_n$ is suitable as a ground-truth for empirical experiments: given $n$, $\bar{v}$, and $\tau$, a respondent's choice between red and blue reveals whether their $w_0/w$ ratio exceeds the threshold.
